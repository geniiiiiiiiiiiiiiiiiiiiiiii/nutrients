Section 01

What Actually Is a Calorie?

A calorie (lowercase c) is a physics term: the energy required to raise 1 gram of water by 1°C. That's tiny. What you see on food labels is actually a kilocalorie (kcal) — 1,000 of those small calories — though we lazily call it a "calorie" in everyday speech.

Think of a calorie not as a "thing" in food, but as a measurement — the way kilometres measure distance. You're measuring how much chemical energy is locked inside a food's molecular bonds, waiting to be released.

🔬 The physics: When your cells break down glucose (sugar), they're doing the same thing as burning it — releasing the energy stored in carbon-hydrogen bonds. Your body just does it in a controlled, step-by-step way to capture that energy as ATP, rather than releasing it all at once as heat.

Section 02

Where Do Calories Actually Go?

Your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is not just "what you burn at the gym." It's the sum of four completely different processes happening at the same time:

🛌 BMR — Basal Metabolic Rate
68%
🚶 NEAT — Daily Movement
17%
🍽 TEF — Digesting Food
10%
🏋️ EAT — Exercise
5%

BMR is huge and often underestimated. Even if you did absolutely nothing all day — no movement, no digestion, lying still in a coma-like state — your body burns 1,400–1,900 kcal just to keep organs running: heart pumping, lungs inflating, kidneys filtering, neurons firing, cells dividing.

💡 Surprising fact: Your brain alone consumes roughly 300–400 kcal per day, about 20% of your BMR, despite being only 2% of your body weight. On exam days or periods of intense focus, this can increase slightly. This is why mental fatigue feels physical — it is.

NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis) is wildly underrated. Fidgeting, typing, standing, walking to the kitchen — it all adds up. Naturally "lean" people often unconsciously move more throughout the day (up to 350 extra kcal/day in studies). This is why two people eating the same amount can have very different body compositions.

TEF (Thermic Effect of Food) means digestion burns calories too. Protein has the highest TEF at 20–30% of its caloric value — meaning eating 100 kcal of protein effectively "costs" 20–30 kcal to process. This is one reason high-protein diets aid fat loss.

Section 04

The Calorie Spectrum: What Really Happens

Your body doesn't just "store fat" or "burn fat" in a binary way. Caloric intake creates a spectrum of physiological responses, and the extremes are very different from a moderate deficit or surplus.

🚨 Starvation
< TDEE − 1000

Muscle catabolism, organ stress, immune crash, metabolic adaptation, hair loss, hormonal shutdown

📉 Deficit
TDEE − 250/500

Fat loss, mild hunger, possible energy dip. Sustainable and healthy if protein is adequate

✅ Maintenance
= TDEE

Weight stable, energy consistent, body in homeostasis. Ideal for long-term health

📈 Surplus
TDEE + 250/500

Weight gain. With training, supports muscle growth. Fat gain is minimal if controlled

🚨 Excess
> TDEE + 1000

Rapid fat gain, insulin resistance, inflammation, metabolic syndrome risk over time

Let's look at what really happens inside at each extreme:

What happens in a moderate deficit (−500 kcal/day) over time:

  • Your body pulls energy from stored triglycerides (body fat) — cells literally shrink as fat is mobilized and oxidised
  • Insulin levels drop, enabling fat cells to release their stored fatty acids into the bloodstream
  • Growth hormone spikes slightly to preserve muscle tissue
  • Mild hunger hormone ghrelin increases — you'll feel hungrier, especially in weeks 1–2
  • After 3–4 weeks, metabolic adaptation begins: BMR can drop ~5–10% as your body tries to conserve energy
  • Lose ~0.5 kg/week (mostly fat if protein intake is high, around 1.6–2.2g per kg bodyweight)
  • Cognitive effects are minimal at moderate deficits — you may actually feel clearer at first

What happens at maintenance calories day-to-day:

  • Glucose is prioritised as fuel; glycogen stores in liver (~100g) and muscles (~400g) stay topped up
  • No fat mobilisation needed — the body is in energy equilibrium
  • Insulin cycles normally with meals; glucose cleared efficiently
  • Hormones like leptin, testosterone, and thyroid hormones are at optimal levels
  • Immune system gets full energy allocation for maintenance and response
  • Tissue repair happens at normal rate — this is the body's "default" mode
  • Sleep quality tends to be better; cortisol rhythms stay normal

What happens in a lean bulk (+250–500 kcal/day):

  • Glycogen stores are fully topped up first — liver and muscle glycogen hit maximum capacity
  • Insulin stays elevated longer; glucose is converted to fatty acids via de novo lipogenesis
  • With resistance training + protein: the surplus fuels muscle protein synthesis (MPS)
  • IGF-1 and mTOR pathway activation — the molecular signals that drive muscle growth
  • Expect ~0.5–1 kg total weight gain/month on a proper lean bulk (about 50% muscle, 50% fat)
  • Energy levels are high; recovery from training is noticeably faster
  • Long-term uncontrolled surplus leads to increased visceral fat, which is metabolically active in a bad way

What happens in severe restriction (<1000 kcal/day):

  • Glycogen depletes in 24–48 hours; the body switches to gluconeogenesis — making glucose from muscle proteins
  • Cortisol spikes chronically to break down muscle (catabolism) for fuel — you lose muscle fast
  • Ketone bodies rise as the liver begins converting fat into an alternative brain fuel
  • T3 thyroid hormone drops 20–40% — metabolism slows dramatically, you feel constantly cold
  • Leptin (satiety hormone) plummets; you become obsessed with food — this is biological, not weakness
  • Immune function deteriorates; wound healing slows; hair growth halts (telogen effluvium)
  • Refeeding too fast after starvation can cause refeeding syndrome — dangerous electrolyte shifts
Section 05

Myth vs. Reality

Calories are one of the most myth-laden topics in nutrition. Here are the most common misconceptions — and the real science:

Myth "Eating at night makes you fat"
Your body doesn't have a clock that decides "it's 9pm, store everything as fat." What matters is your total 24-hour caloric balance, not timing. The reason late eating correlates with weight gain is behavioural: people tend to eat more calories at night (snacking, alcohol, larger portions), not because night-time is metabolically different.
Myth "A calorie is a calorie — food quality doesn't matter"
Technically true at the level of energy balance, but wildly misleading in practice. 500 kcal of white sugar and 500 kcal of chicken + vegetables have completely different effects on hunger hormones, nutrient delivery, gut microbiome, and long-term health. Calories matter for weight; food quality matters for everything else.
Myth "You must burn 3,500 kcal to lose 1 pound of fat"
This is a rough and outdated rule. In the short term, it somewhat holds — but it ignores metabolic adaptation, water retention changes, and the different energy costs of losing fat vs. muscle. Real weight loss is non-linear; the first week often shows 2–3 kg loss (mostly water/glycogen), followed by slower true fat loss.
Myth "Eating more meals speeds up your metabolism"
The "6 small meals" idea was widely promoted but doesn't hold up. Total TEF is determined by total food eaten, not meal frequency. Eating 6 x 300 kcal meals produces the same metabolic effect as 3 x 600 kcal meals. Meal frequency is about personal preference and hunger management, not metabolism boosting.
Fact Food labels can be off by up to 20%
The FDA allows ±20% error on food label calorie claims. Meanwhile, most people underestimate their portion sizes by 20–40%. This is why meticulous calorie counting rarely produces exact predicted results. Use calorie data as a guide, not a perfect measurement — and adjust based on real-world results over 2–3 weeks.

Section 07

How to Actually Use This Knowledge

Calorie knowledge is most powerful when it becomes intuition, not obsession. Here's how to apply it:

01
Don't count — estimate. You don't need a food scale forever. Learn rough calorie ranges for the foods you eat most. After 2–3 weeks of tracking, most people develop a reliable intuition that lasts years.
02
Watch liquid calories. Juices, smoothies, lattes, alcohol — they don't trigger the same satiety signals as solid food. A 400 kcal coffee drink doesn't make you less hungry at lunch. Solid food does.
03
Move more throughout the day (NEAT). A gym session burns 250–400 kcal. Being an active, fidgety person all day burns 300–600 kcal. The latter requires zero effort. Take the stairs, walk while on calls, stand sometimes.
04
Never go below ~1,400–1,600 kcal. Below this threshold, you lose muscle rapidly, metabolism adapts downward, and the deficit becomes unsustainable. Slow and steady (−300 to −500 kcal/day) beats aggressive restriction every time.
05
Recalculate every 5–7 kg. As your weight changes, your BMR changes. A person at 90 kg has a higher TDEE than the same person at 75 kg — so the calorie target that worked before needs adjusting.

Today's Takeaways

The 5 Things to Remember

→
Calories measure energy — carbs & protein give 4 kcal/g, fat 9 kcal/g, alcohol 7 kcal/g.
→
BMR dominates — ~65–70% of your calories are burned just keeping you alive. Exercise is a small piece.
→
NEAT is massive — unconscious daily movement can account for 300–600 extra kcal/day and varies wildly between people.
→
Deficits should be moderate — −300 to −500 kcal/day is sustainable and muscle-preserving. Extreme restriction backfires.
→
Food quality still matters — calories determine weight, but what those calories are made of determines everything else about your health.
